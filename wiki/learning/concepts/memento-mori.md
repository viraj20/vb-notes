# Memento mori

Parent: [[../index|Learning MOC]]

Latin: "remember you must die." The deliberate practice of keeping mortality in view — not morbidly, but as a calibration tool for what matters.

## The move: death as teacher, not threat

Morrie Schwartz, in [[../books/tuesdays-with-morrie|Tuesdays With Morrie]]:

> "Once you learn how to die, you learn how to live." (loc 847)
>
> "Every day, have a little bird on your shoulder that asks, 'Is today the day? Am I ready? Am I doing all I need to do? Am I being the person I want to be?'" (loc 839)

The mechanism is simple: imminent mortality makes status games look absurd and love-and-meaning decisions look obvious. Morrie: "Status will get you nowhere." (loc 1317)

## Not the same as nihilism

Frankl ([[../books/mans-search-for-meaning|Man's Search for Meaning]]) makes the distinction explicit: confronting death can *either* flatten meaning ("nothing matters, so why bother") or intensify it ("this is finite, so it must mean something"). Logotherapy is the second move.

Brown's [[../books/origin|Origin]] captures the Nietzschean version: once "God is dead," the question of how to live without denying death becomes central (loc 4197–4208).

## Contrast: fear of ageing > fear of dying

Backman ([[../books/and-every-morning-the-way-home|And Every Morning…]]): "our fear of getting old seems to be even greater than our fear of dying." (loc 77)

Morrie's counter (loc 1223): "Aging is not just decay. It's growth. It's more than the negative that you're going to die, it's also the positive that you understand you're going to die, and that you live a better life because of it."

## Applied

- **On-call / incident work.** The operational version: every outage is a small memento — systems die, companies die, services die. The question is what you were doing with them.
- **Career.** "Is this the thing I want on my headstone?" is a sharper question than "is this the thing I want on my LinkedIn?"
