# Social vs market norms

Parent: [[../index|Learning MOC]] · Source: [[../books/predictably-irrational|Predictably Irrational]] (Ariely)

## The distinction

Two parallel sets of rules govern human exchange:

- **Social norms** — friends help friends move. A good neighbor watches the kids. You bring wine to a dinner party. Exchange is reciprocal, diffuse, and based on relationship, not equivalence.
- **Market norms** — wages, prices, contracts, receipts. Exchange is immediate, tightly specified, and based on calculation.

Each works fine on its own. The problem is mixing them.

## The one-way door

> "Once a social norm is trumped by a market norm, it will rarely return." — Ariely (loc 1131)

The canonical experiment: if you introduce a small fine for parents picking kids up late at daycare, late pickups *increase*. The fine converts a social obligation ("I shouldn't inconvenience the teachers") into a market transaction ("I'm paying for the extension"). Removing the fine doesn't restore the original norm — the relationship has been repriced.

## Implications for teams

- **Money is the expensive motivator** — "MONEY is very often the most expensive way to motivate people. Social norms are not only cheaper, but often more effective." (loc 1245)
- **Paying per task destroys intrinsic motivation** — once a bonus is tied to a behavior, the behavior becomes transactional. Removing the bonus doesn't restore the unpaid version; it creates resentment.
- **A mission-driven culture leverages social norms** — Sinek's [[golden-circle|WHY]] is economically rational *because* it accesses a cheaper, more durable coordination mechanism than money.
- **Be careful introducing pricing** — charging for things previously free (office coffee, overtime comp) can convert the whole relationship from social to market without anyone noticing until it's too late.
