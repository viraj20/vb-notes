# First-impression signals

Parent: [[../index|Learning MOC]]

The observation that counterparties form a read in the first few seconds, before conscious evaluation, from a small number of body and voice signals. [[../books/how-to-talk-to-anyone|Lowndes]] (loc 239): "emotional reactions occur even before the brain has had time to register what's causing that reaction." This page distils her 90+ tactics into a smaller, usable shortlist.

The skill is neutral — the same signals that open a conversation with a stranger can be used to manipulate or to genuinely engage. [[inspire-vs-manipulate|Inspire vs manipulate]] is the ethical calibration; [[mr-fix-it-trap|Mr. Fix-It trap]] is the listening-vs-solving prerequisite.

## The load-bearing four

If only four signals are worth remembering from Lowndes, these are them:

### 1. Flooding smile (not instant smile)

Lowndes (loc 303, 304): don't flash an immediate smile. Look at the face for a beat, then let the smile spread. The delay reads as *only for them* — if the smile is instant, it was already en route; if it's delayed, it was triggered by their specific presence.

### 2. Sticky eyes (slow release)

Loc 357, 358: don't break eye contact the instant they finish speaking. Hold it for an extra beat, release slowly. Signals sustained interest without the intensity of unbroken gaze.

### 3. Total-body turn

Loc 515: pivot 100% toward the new person. Not a head-turn; a torso-turn. Signals undivided attention at a physical level that the counterparty feels before they name it.

### 4. Hang-by-your-teeth posture

Loc 435: head high, shoulders back, torso out of hips, feet weightless. A one-phrase mnemonic for confident carriage. Most of what the counterparty reads as "this person is senior" is some version of this.

## The amplifiers (four more, sustained rather than first-moment)

### Mood match

Loc 809: before speaking, take a "voice sample" — what is their current state? Match it first, then steer. Jumping into high energy with a tired counterparty reads as imposition; matching first earns the right to steer.

### Comm-YOU-nication

Loc 1509: lead sentences with *you* rather than *I*. "YOU look great in that suit" > "I like your suit." "Boss, can YOU do without me Friday?" > "Can I take Friday off?" Pushes the pride button and saves the listener the translation step.

### Echoing

Loc 2228: use *their* nouns, verbs, prepositions — not yours. Creates subliminal rapport because the listener hears their own mental model returning through your mouth.

### Limit the fidget

Loc 621: no face-touching, scratching, collar-adjusting when communication matters. Face-hand contact reads as fibbing at a cue-level your counterparty doesn't consciously parse.

## What to skip from Lowndes

- **Epoxy eyes** (loc 392, 401) — Lowndes recommends sustained unblinking gaze "especially effective on women if they find you attractive" on the theory they'll mistake their nervous response for infatuation. This is reading autonomic arousal as romantic interest — bad epistemics, unethical deployment. Don't.
- **The "85% of success is communication skills" statistic** (loc 1266) is folk. The underlying spirit is plausible; the number isn't research.
- **Gendered framings** throughout — de-gender when reusing. Same move as with Gray's [[mr-fix-it-trap|Mr. Fix-It trap]].

## Why these signals matter at all

Two mechanisms:

1. **Halo effect.** A strong first impression creates a positive prior that subsequent behavior is evaluated against. You get credit you haven't earned yet. A bad first impression creates the opposite tax.
2. **Affordance for the other party.** Good signals make it *easier* for the counterparty to engage with you. Bad signals raise their cost. The tools are less about you looking good and more about you being low-cost to interact with.

## For engineering contexts

### Interviews (both sides)

The first 30 seconds of an interview set the tone for the next 45 minutes. Flooding smile + total-body turn + hang-by-your-teeth will elevate the signal-to-noise of any technical interview. For candidates: it makes the interviewer's job of evaluating you *easier*, which benefits you. For interviewers: it lowers the anxiety the candidate is spending bandwidth on, so you get more signal on the technical questions.

### Cross-team first meetings

You're meeting the security team / platform team / product partner for the first time. The signals front-load trust so the later substantive discussion starts from a better prior. Mood-match especially — security teams are often over-stressed and under-resourced; leading with high energy imposes; matching first earns the right to propose.

### On-call handoff

Limit-the-fidget and echoing are the two sustained signals that matter. The outgoing on-call is stressed; your body language determines whether they hand off cleanly or protectively. Mirror their pace before you try to move faster.

### Post-incident meetings

Mood match first. Then you can introduce corrective energy. Opening briskly when the room is in a sombre mode — "Okay, action items" — feels to the room like their experience is being skipped.
