# Three sources of meaning (Frankl)

Parent: [[../index|Learning MOC]] · Source: [[../books/mans-search-for-meaning|Man's Search for Meaning]]

## The three sources

Frankl, distilled in the foreword summary (loc 63):

1. **Work** — doing something significant. Purposeful activity toward an outcome that matters.
2. **Love** — caring for another person. Frankl held his wife's image through Auschwitz; the relationship was meaningful whether or not she was physically present (loc 577).
3. **Courage in difficult times** — how you respond when circumstances are intolerable.

## The crucial inversion

Suffering in itself is meaningless. **Meaning comes from the stance you take toward it.** This is logotherapy's core departure from Freudian/Adlerian psychology — meaning isn't a pleasure or a power; it's the act of response.

> "You cannot control what happens to you in life, but you can always control what you will feel and do about what happens to you." (loc 71)

## Related to adjacent concepts

- [[flow-state|Flow state]] — Ikigai connects flow to ikigai; Frankl connects meaning to response. The two frames overlap in "work" as a source of meaning.
- [[../books/ikigai|Ikigai]] explicitly cites Frankl — "He who has a why can live with almost any how."
- Discovery, not creation — we don't *create* meaning, we *discover* it. Matches Ikigai's framing.

## For engineering teams

Frankl's three sources translate loosely to team design:

- **Work** — give people something significant to work toward (not just on; see [[../books/start-with-why|Sinek]]).
- **Love** — the "circle of safety" in [[../books/leaders-eat-last|Leaders Eat Last]] is the team-scale analogue.
- **Courage in difficult times** — incident response, on-call, layoffs: the response is where identity forms.
