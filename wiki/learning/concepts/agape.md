# Agape

Parent: [[../index|Learning MOC]]

Greek: ἀγάπη — love as committed action and will, distinct from *eros* (desire) and *philia* (friendship). In Christian theology, agape is the unconditional, self-giving love.

## Coelho's framing in [[../books/the-pilgrimage|The Pilgrimage]]

Petrus, Coelho's RAM-tradition master, makes agape one of three tests of wisdom (loc 305):

> "The true path to wisdom can be identified by three things: first, it must involve agape… second, it has to have practical application… and finally, it has to be a path that can be followed by anyone."

Two practical consequences of centring agape:

- **Love of one's enemy is part of agape** (loc 2169). The enemy is a test of your "grip, will, and handling of the sword." Fleeing is worse than losing. Contrast [[../books/the-48-laws-of-power|Greene]]: crush the enemy.
- **Dreams are nourished by agape** (loc 613). "If we don't dream, our soul dies, and agape cannot reach it." Agape is the channel, not the destination.

## Frankl's implicit agape

[[three-sources-of-meaning|Frankl's second source of meaning is love]]. In the camps, Frankl held his wife's image as a source of meaning whether or not she was physically alive. That's agape in Coelho's sense — love as committed stance, not transactional feeling.

## Why this concept matters

Most "love" in everyday usage collapses agape, eros, and philia. When [[../books/tuesdays-with-morrie|Morrie]] says "love is the only rational act" (loc 565), the word has to be read as agape — or the sentence is nonsense.

For engineering management, agape is the active-verb version of Sinek's "circle of safety" ([[../books/leaders-eat-last|Leaders Eat Last]]).
