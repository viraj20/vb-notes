# Divine proportion (φ, 1.618)

Parent: [[../index|Learning MOC]]

The golden ratio: the number φ = (1 + √5) / 2 ≈ 1.6180339… A mathematical constant appearing in sequences defined by self-similarity, most famously the Fibonacci ratio limit.

## What [[../books/the-da-vinci-code|The Da Vinci Code]] popularises

Langdon's classroom demonstration (loc 1600–1623):

- Height ÷ (belly button to floor) ≈ 1.618
- Shoulder-to-fingertips ÷ elbow-to-fingertips ≈ 1.618
- Hip-to-floor ÷ knee-to-floor ≈ 1.618
- The pentacle's line-segment ratios = φ
- Da Vinci's Vitruvian Man (loc 805) as the iconic expression

Brown's framing: humans are "walking tributes to the Divine Proportion." (loc 1603)

## What's mathematically true

- φ is the positive solution of x² = x + 1. Equivalently, the ratio where a/b = (a+b)/a.
- It is the limit of consecutive Fibonacci ratios.
- It appears in phyllotaxis (leaf arrangements), sunflower-seed spirals, pinecone scales — because packing self-similar elements around a central point is most efficient at φ-based angles (137.5°).

## What's oversold

Most claimed φ-in-nature-or-art measurements are loose, cherry-picked, or coincidental:

- Human-body ratios show wide distributions centred only loosely around φ; the "Vitruvian proof" overstates the precision.
- The Parthenon, Great Pyramid, Mona Lisa, and nautilus shell all have φ-claims attached that mostly don't survive careful measurement.
- Brown is writing fiction; the novel's "PHI appears everywhere" line is symbology-as-plot-device, not empirical claim.

## Why the idea endures

The ratio *does* appear in places governed by self-similar growth. Our pattern-matching system then over-extends it to places it doesn't truly govern. That over-extension is the thing to resist — the base idea remains elegant.
