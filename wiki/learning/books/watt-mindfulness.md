# A Practical Guide to Mindfulness — Tessa Watt

Parent: [[../index|Learning MOC]]

**Short page, one concrete exercise.** 2 highlights, one of them a complete mindfulness exercise worth keeping.

## The load-bearing exercise: panning-camera scan

Watt, loc 1083:

> "Wherever you are – in your house, on a train, in a café – take a few moments to look around. You could scan the scene as if you were a film camera, panning slowly from left to right, and back again. Let your eyes take in whatever is here – objects, people, colours, shapes, textures, light and shade. See if you can notice your reactions. Are there certain colours, objects or people you feel attracted to – things you naturally like? Are there other things you find unpleasant and would like to push away? Is there a third category of things you feel neutral about, perhaps don't even notice when you first look?"

The exercise trains the [[../concepts/hemispheric-attention|right-mode / broad-vigilant]] attention McGilchrist describes — *noticing* rather than *categorising*. Three things the exercise produces:

1. A deliberate slowing — the camera pans, it doesn't jump.
2. Named reactions — attracted / repelled / neutral — surfacing what usually runs below awareness.
3. A third-category check — what am I *not seeing*? The neutral category is the biggest and usually invisible.

## For engineering contexts

The panning-camera exercise maps directly to on-call situational awareness. When a page arrives, the reflex is to jump to the specific alert. The mindful move is to *pan* the dashboard first: what am I *not* being alerted on that looks wrong? What am I habitually ignoring because it's always yellow? The neutral-category check is the highest-value move — it's where unnoticed failure modes live.

Also applies to architecture review: first pan, then focus. See [[../concepts/hemispheric-attention|hemispheric attention]] for the general frame.

## Not covered here

The book's broader material (formal meditation practice, body scans, mindful eating, MBSR) was not captured in the highlights. For sustained practice, pair with a specific tradition — this book alone is insufficient.
