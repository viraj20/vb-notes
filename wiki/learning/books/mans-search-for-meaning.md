# Man's Search for Meaning (Frankl)

- Author: Viktor E. Frankl
- ASIN: B00EKOC0HI
- Source: Kindle highlights (5 annotations — the core thesis is compact)

Parent: [[../index|Learning MOC]] · Related: [[ikigai|Ikigai]] (explicitly cites Frankl) · [[sophies-world|Sophie's World]]

## Core argument

Life is a quest for **meaning** — not pleasure (Freud) or power (Adler). Meaning is the primary psychological need, and it can be found in any circumstance, even in a concentration camp.

## Frankl's three sources of meaning

From the foreword summary (loc 63). See [[../concepts/three-sources-of-meaning|Three sources of meaning]].

1. **Work** — doing something significant.
2. **Love** — caring for another person (Frankl held onto his wife's image through Auschwitz).
3. **Courage** in difficult times — how you respond to suffering.

Suffering in itself is meaningless; meaning comes from the stance you take toward it.

## The last freedom

> "Everything can be taken from a man but one thing, your freedom to choose how you will respond to the situation. You cannot control what happens to you in life, but you can always control what you will feel and do about what happens to you." (loc 71)

[[ikigai|Ikigai]] quotes this almost verbatim.

## Love transcends presence

> "Love goes very far beyond the physical person of the beloved. It finds its deepest meaning in his spiritual being … whether or not he is still alive at all, ceases somehow to be of importance." (loc 577)

## Logotherapy vs psychoanalysis

Frankl's school — we don't *create* meaning; we *discover* it. This is the inversion that [[ikigai|Ikigai]]'s chapter on purpose builds on.

## Other notable passages

**Human duality — the same species built Auschwitz and walked into it with prayer.** Frankl refuses both sentimentality and cynicism; the realism of post-war humanism is holding both facts at once. "Man is that being who invented the gas chambers of Auschwitz; however, he is also that being who entered those gas chambers upright, with the Lord's Prayer or the Shema Yisrael on his lips" (loc 91).

**The psychology of arrival — self-protective illusion at the threshold of catastrophe.** Newly-arrived prisoners clung to a belief they would somehow be spared, a mechanism Frankl names and uses to explain why clear-eyed acceptance is itself a late-stage achievement: the "delusion of reprieve" (loc 223).

**Love as the terminal value.** Across the book's middle arc Frankl lands on love not as one good among many but as the ceiling of human striving — the frame against which work and courage are instrumental. "The truth — that love is the ultimate and the highest goal to which man can aspire" (loc 563).
