# Catching Fire (Collins, Book 2)

Parent: [[../index|Learning MOC]]

Book 2 of the Hunger Games trilogy. VB captured 13 highlights — middle-book structural beats, Katniss interrogating her own motives, the pivot from victim to symbol.

## The central question Katniss asks herself (loc 1260)

Of the poisonous berries she held out to Peeta at the end of Book 1:

- "If I held them out to save Peeta because I knew I would be shunned if I came back without him, then I am despicable.
- If I held them out because I loved him, I am still self-centered, although forgivable.
- But if I held them out to defy the Capitol, I am someone of worth.
- The trouble is, I don't know exactly what was going on inside me at that moment."

Collins's structural move: the reader learns the protagonist's motive by watching her *interrogate* her own, not by being told. Compare [[turtles-all-the-way-down|John Green's]] OCD narrator who doubts her own thoughts the same way.

## Facing rather than fleeing (loc 1266)

> "Life in District 12 isn't really so different from life in the arena. At some point, you have to stop running and turn around and face whoever wants you dead."

Echoes [[the-pilgrimage|Coelho's Petrus]] on not fleeing the fight. The trilogy's political arc is this line literalised.

## The turn to symbolism (loc 2542)

> "If I can make it clear that I'm still defying the Capitol right up to the end, the Capitol will have killed me… but not my spirit. What better way to give hope to the rebels?"

Katniss discovers that her death can be more subversive than her survival — the logic that makes mockingjay iconography possible in Book 3.

## Aphorisms

> "Sometimes things happen to people and they're not equipped to deal with them." (loc 339)

> "Having an eye for beauty isn't the same thing as a weakness." (loc 2204)

## Other notable passages

District 12's texture is established through named background figures — the small-cast scaffolding Collins uses to make the village feel lived-in before the plot uproots it: Cray the Head Peacekeeper, Greasy Sae the hob cook, and the Peacekeeper Darius who will later pay for kindness (loc 116, loc 119, loc 120).

Katniss reads her own body as evidence of rupture — the unchanged lake versus the Capitol-smoothed skin, Gale's scarred and capable hands versus her own erased ones — physical detail doing the work of interior monologue (loc 370, loc 1022).

The finale inverts Peeta's earlier self-protective creed into public weaponry: his line about channelling emotion into work so he hurts only himself (loc 2651) collides with the televised "baby" lie (loc 2680) and the private vow to spend every remaining minute together (loc 2549) — three registers of the same man turning his pain outward to move an audience.
