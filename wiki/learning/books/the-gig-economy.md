# The Gig Economy (Mulcahy)

- Author: Diane Mulcahy
- ASIN: B01HJ35YPG
- Source: Kindle highlights (17 annotations)

Parent: [[../index|Learning MOC]] · Related: [[the-100-startup|The $100 Startup]] · [[the-millionaire-fastlane|Millionaire Fastlane]] · [[../../finances/index|Finances MOC]]

## Core argument

The "employee in a full-time job" model is eroding; people increasingly work across a portfolio of consulting, contracting, freelancing, part-time, and side gigs. Mulcahy's ten-rule playbook reframes what "success" and "security" mean when the job-for-life no longer exists.

## Mulcahy's ten rules (loc 133)

1. **Define your success** — personal vision, not the inherited American Dream.
2. **Diversify** — multiple gigs compound opportunity, skill, and network.
3. **Create your own security** — no employer provides it; build income security, exit strategy, safety net yourself.
4. **Connect without networking** — inbound or outbound; master the ask and the offer.
5. **Face fear by reducing risk** — break big fears into manageable risks.
6. **Take time off between gigs** — and make it meaningful.
7. **Be mindful about time** — Maker vs Manager schedule; calendar as identity.
8. **Be financially flexible** — reject the save-your-latte-money approach; restructure for flexibility.
9. **Think access, not ownership** — rent/access what you need; avoid debt-locked possessions.
10. **Save for a traditional retirement, but don't plan on having one** — the question is *when can I stop working*, not *when can I retire*.

## Key ideas extracted

- **Gig Economy scope** (loc 98) — consulting, contractors, part-time, temp, freelance, self-employed, side gigs, platform work.
- **Workers want autonomy, flexibility, meaning** (loc 113) — not just higher pay.
- **Full-time jobs are the worker of last choice** for many companies now (loc 161). Structural shift, not a phase.
- **Inherited definitions of success** — "we can all too easily fall into living a life based on the priorities of others" (loc 301). Echoes [[the-millionaire-fastlane|DeMarco's]] "faux wealth."
- **Regret inversion** (loc 364) — "I wish I'd had the courage to live a life true to myself, not what others expected of me." Bronnie Ware's top-five deathbed regret.
- **The Dash** (loc 388) — "what matters is how we live and love and how we spend our dash." The dash between birth and death dates on a tombstone.
- **Right amount of time** — "we can accomplish more of our biggest and most meaningful goals if we give ourselves the right amount of time." (loc 477)
- **Opportunity mindset** — "What work can I do, and what value can I bring?" (loc 489) Inverts the scarcity question "will there be jobs?"

## Other notable passages

**Income is bimodal, not uniformly better.** Going independent is not a monotonic pay raise — the distribution widens in both directions, and autonomy is the more reliable gain than compensation. "Wages are stagnating, and while working independently can generate higher earnings, it can also result in lower compensation" (loc 111); many independents cite "autonomy, flexibility, and greater control... and in many cases they earn more" (loc 119).

**Naming the default model is half the escape.** Mulcahy repeatedly labels the incumbent arrangement — the "employee-in-a-job model" (loc 116) — so that its erosion becomes legible rather than felt only as anxiety. Status-signaling follows the same logic: we notice "beckon" moments (loc 234) more than quiet command, so gig-era reputation must be engineered deliberately.

**Personal-vision work is a quieting practice, not an aspiration exercise.** Mulcahy treats clarity about success as what remains after the external voices stop — "Only when we quiet the peer, parental, academic, corporate, and social voices telling us what we 'should' want... can we listen for our own internal desires" (loc 307). The prescription is deliberately undemanding: "simply experience and bring forth what you are and what you might be" (loc 314), "write them down... get in touch with your own thoughts" (loc 316), and treat it as "a time of creative incubation. You just see what happens" (loc 319).
