# The Hunger Games (Collins, Book 1)

Parent: [[../index|Learning MOC]]

First book of Suzanne Collins's dystopian YA trilogy. VB captured 12 highlights — mostly world-building and Katniss's political awakening. Book page is compact; sequels ([[catching-fire|Catching Fire]], [[mockingjay|Mockingjay]]) carry most of the thematic weight.

## The premise, in the words captured

- **Panem's origin** (loc 185): rose from the ashes of North America after "the disasters, the droughts, the storms, the fires, the encroaching seas that swallowed up so much of the land, the brutal war for what little sustenance remained."
- **The Games as terror** (loc 190): each district sends one boy and one girl as "tributes" to fight to the death. The public spectacle is the punishment.
- **District 12** (loc 58, 427): "Where you can starve to death in safety." Appalachian coal country.

## Silence as dissent (loc 247)

> "Instead of acknowledging applause, I stand there unmoving while they take part in the boldest form of dissent they can manage. Silence. Which says we do not agree. We do not condone. All of this is wrong."

A recurring motif across the trilogy: the authoritarian regime has made every overt act of protest dangerous, so dissent finds the forms it can — silence, a three-finger salute, a mockingjay pin. The same pattern appears in [[origin|Brown's]] faith-vs-science dialectic: symbols matter because they're what remains when speech is constrained.

## The spark

> "Only I keep wishing I could think of a way to… show the Capitol they don't own me. That I'm more than just a piece in their Games." (loc 2395)

This line seeds the whole trilogy's arc — the berries, the mockingjay, the Games turning inward onto the Capitol. See [[catching-fire|Catching Fire]] for the pivot.

## Aphorisms

> "I am not pretty. I am not beautiful. I am as radiant as the sun." (loc 1229)

## Other notable passages

Small gestures carry the political weight the regime has stripped from speech. Gale's woodland rants about the tesserae lottery (loc 142) and the district's shared three-finger salute — "it means thanks, it means admiration, it means good-bye to someone you love" (loc 251) — show how dissent relocates into private vocabulary when public speech is policed.

Rue's meadow lullaby is the book's one sustained lyric, and Collins lets it play twice — first as Katniss sings Rue to sleep (loc 2373), then as the melody fades to barely audible final lines (loc 2380). The repetition turns a child's song into the book's closest thing to a prayer: safety and warmth imagined only in hiding.

The single-word highlight "ludicrous" (loc 1160) is the smallest marker but reads as Katniss's running register for the Capitol's aesthetics — the prep teams, the fashion, the spectacle — a quiet counterweight to the terror of loc 190.
