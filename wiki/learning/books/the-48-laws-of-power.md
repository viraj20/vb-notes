# The 48 Laws of Power (Greene)

- Author: Robert Greene
- ASIN: B0041G68Z0
- Source: Kindle highlights (11 annotations — mostly introductory laws)

Parent: [[../index|Learning MOC]] · Related: [[start-with-why|Start With Why]] · [[leaders-eat-last|Leaders Eat Last]] · [[how-to-win-friends-and-influence-people|Carnegie]]

## Core argument

Power is a social game with ancient rules. Most people play reactively, bound by emotion and conventional morality; Greene catalogs 48 laws that power-literate operators actually follow. The tone is Machiavellian — descriptive, not aspirational.

## Key ideas extracted

- **Trying to be good all the time is ruinous** — "Any man who tries to be good all the time is bound to come to ruin among the great number who are not good." (loc 352). Echoes Machiavelli.
- **Emotions cloud reason** (loc 416) — the player who stays cool retains option space.
- **Wargame your plan, don't romance it** — "calculate every possible permutation and pitfall" (loc 432). Contrasts sharply with planning theatre.
- **Study people, not herbs** — "how much more important it would be to study those of people, with whom we must live or die!" (loc 480).
- **Never coast on favor** — "Never take your position for granted and never let any favors you receive go to your head." (loc 618)
- **Selective cruelty toward superiors** — when a superior is a falling star, don't be merciful (loc 640). This sits uneasily next to [[leaders-eat-last|Sinek]] — the frames conflict.
- **Friends are more dangerous than enemies** — "Lord, protect me from my friends; I can take care of my enemies." (loc 721). Greene's explanation: friends become resentful; enemies know where they stand.
- **Lincoln's inversion** — "Why, madam, do I not destroy my enemies when I make them my friends?" (loc 780). The magnanimity alternative.
- **Men repay injury over benefit** — gratitude is a burden, revenge a pleasure (loc 786).
- **Something-to-prove energy** — "A person who has something to prove will move mountains for you." (loc 819). Useful for hiring and delegation.

## Tensions with adjacent reading

- Greene vs [[start-with-why|Sinek]]: Sinek treats trust as the compounding asset of leadership; Greene treats it as exploitable. Both can be true at different scales — Sinek's frame for building; Greene's for surviving hostile political environments.
- Greene vs [[how-to-win-friends-and-influence-people|Carnegie]]: Carnegie builds influence through sincere interest; Greene through studied indirection.
